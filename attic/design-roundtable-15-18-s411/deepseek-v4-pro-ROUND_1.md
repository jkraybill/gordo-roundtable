# Findings: Question-Type Classification + Dissent Capture

## Feature A: Question-Type Classification

**Recommendation:** Implement, but minimally.

The classification axis (convergent/divergent/design) is genuinely useful — it shapes what "good process" means and what synthesis should optimize for. This isn't taxonomy for taxonomy's sake; it changes the teleology of the roundtable.

**Minimal viable approach:** Convener-specified only. One required field before roundtable launch. Default to "convergent" with a brief tooltip explaining the distinction.

**Rationale for keeping it simple now:**
- Auto-detection is a research problem dressed as a feature. Question structure correlates weakly with question type; semantic content matters more, and that's hard to get right without false confidence.
- A hybrid approach (auto-suggest, human confirm) sounds appealing but adds UX friction and a failure mode (wrong suggestion accepted) without clear evidence the detection works.
- Convener specification has the additional benefit of making the convener *explicitly commit* to what they're asking for, which is itself deliberatively useful.

**Defer to post-v1.0:** Auto-detection once you have a corpus of labeled conference data from actual usage. The classifier will be trainable then; before that, you're guessing.

**Dissent on one point:** I'm not confident the three categories are complete. "Convergent" and "divergent" form a clean spectrum, but "design" feels like it might be a subtype of convergent (where the convergence is toward a *decision procedure* rather than a *factual claim*). I'd watch whether users reliably distinguish these or whether "convergent" does the work. If confusion persists, collapse to a binary axis and let design emerge as convergent-with-tradeoffs.

## Feature B: Dissent Register

**Recommendation:** Implement, but with clear differentiation from existing reasoning_trace.

The gap this fills is real. The transcript shows the *winning* reasoning; the reasoning_trace shows what *this participant* considered. What's missing is the *rejected alternative that multiple participants saw and chose not to pursue* — the near-miss consensus, the path-not-taken that wasn't wrong, just not chosen.

**How it differs from reasoning_trace.concerns_remaining:**

| Aspect | concerns_remaining | dissent_register |
|--------|-------------------|------------------|
| Scope | Individual | Collective |
| Content | Unresolved worries about the *chosen* position | Alternative positions that were seriously considered |
| Signal | "I'm not fully settled" | "We could have gone this other way and here's why we didn't" |
| Temporal stance | Forward-looking (things to watch) | Sideways-looking (the counterfactual) |

They're complementary, not redundant. A concern says "the roof might leak." A dissenting position says "we almost built a different roof entirely, and here's what it would have looked like."

**Implementation suggestion:** Keep it lightweight. A structured field in the roundtable synthesis (not per-participant) that captures: (1) the rejected alternative position, (2) who raised or seriously engaged it, (3) why it wasn't adopted. Generated by the synthesis step, not a separate capture mechanism. This avoids adding participant burden.

## Feature C: Shadow Proposal

**Recommendation:** Over-engineering. Do not implement.

The concept is interesting — capturing the "I thought about saying X but didn't" — but it has structural problems:

1. **Incentive distortion.** Knowing shadow proposals are captured changes what participants choose not to say. Some will performatively shadow-propose to signal sophistication; others will avoid even *thinking* about things they don't want on record. The shadow isn't neutral observation; it's observer effect on internal cognition.

2. **Verifiability vacuum.** There's no way to distinguish "I genuinely considered this and chose silence" from "I'm retroactively claiming I considered this" from "I'm using this as a backchannel to inject a position without defending it." The epistemic value depends on sincerity you can't verify.

3. **Complexity cost for unclear benefit.** This is a novel interaction pattern — participants need to understand it, decide when to use it, and trust how it'll be read. That's a lot of design surface for a pre-v1.0 tool capturing data of uncertain quality.

4. **pass_reflection already partially covers this.** If a participant has genuine doubt about what they *didn't* say, they can surface it there. The gap shadow_proposal fills is narrow: things considered but not said AND not experienced as doubt. That's a thin slice.

**If you're tempted anyway:** Test it as an optional, non-default feature with a clear cost (adds time, adds scrutiny) and see if anyone uses it meaningfully. But I suspect it'll either be ignored or gamed, with little middle ground.

## Interactions

1. **Question type should shape what dissent_register captures.** For convergent questions, the dissent register is most valuable — understanding rejected answers tells you about the epistemic landscape. For divergent questions, dissent is less coherent (dissent from *what*? There's no single answer to contrast with) and you might want a "coverage map" instead. For design questions, the dissent register captures the *alternatives table* — which options were on the table and why they fell off.

2. **Don't make classification gate the features.** If question-type drives behavior but the classification is wrong (convener error), downstream capture becomes misleading. Start with classification being *informational* (displayed, shapes prompts) but not *structural* (doesn't disable or enable capture features). Let the panelists correct misclassification in practice.

3. **dissent_register + reasoning_trace overlap risk.** If both capture similar content in different formats, participants will be confused about where to put things and readers will be confused about where to look. The differentiation I described above works conceptually but needs to survive contact with actual discourse. Watch for conflation in early usage.

4. **The preamble's governance level should influence all of this.** Under "minimal" governance, maybe dissent_register is overkill — just get the answer. Under "full" governance, the dissent register becomes important institutional memory. Consider making these features toggleable by preamble level rather than always-on.
